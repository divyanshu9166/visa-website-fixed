# H-1B RFE Trends in 2026: Why Requests for Evidence Are Rising & How to Respond

If your H-1B petition drew a **Request for Evidence (RFE)** this year, you are far from alone. **H-1B RFE rates have climbed back above 20% in 2026** as USCIS applies stricter, evidence-heavy scrutiny to specialty-occupation petitions. This guide explains what is driving the increase, the most common RFE reasons, how long you have to respond, and how recent 2025–2026 policy changes fit in.

> **Bottom line:** RFEs are up, but they are not denials. Independent analyses of USCIS data show post-RFE approval rates remain strong (mid-80% range) when petitioners submit thorough, well-documented responses.

## Why H-1B RFEs are rising in 2026

Several forces are converging:

- **Tighter adjudication policy.** USCIS is testing not just paperwork but the real-world details of the job, the employer, and the beneficiary's fit — including worksite arrangements, promotions, and salary changes.
- **The weighted selection rule.** On December 23, 2025, DHS finalized a **weighted H-1B selection process** favoring higher-skilled, higher-paid roles, effective **February 27, 2026** for the FY 2027 cap. Wage level is now under a brighter spotlight.
- **The $100,000 proclamation.** A Presidential Proclamation issued **September 19, 2025** requires a **$100,000 payment** for certain H-1B petitions filed on or after **September 21, 2025** (primarily beneficiaries outside the U.S. without a valid H-1B). Petitions lacking proof of payment or an approved exception can be denied outright.
- **Re-examination of extensions.** Even previously approved cases now face fresh scrutiny during extensions and amendments.

## The most common H-1B RFE reasons

| RFE category | What USCIS questions |
| --- | --- |
| **Specialty occupation** | Whether the role genuinely requires a bachelor's degree in a *directly related* specific specialty |
| **Employer-employee relationship** | Whether a valid relationship and right to control the work exist (especially third-party placements) |
| **Beneficiary qualifications** | Whether the worker's degree/experience aligns with the specialty |
| **Wage level adequacy** | Whether the offered wage matches the duties and prevailing wage |
| **Maintenance of status** | Status gaps or documentation issues for in-country beneficiaries |

Specialty occupation and employer-employee relationship RFEs are consistently the most common.

## What USCIS means by "specialty occupation"

Under current USCIS guidance, a specialty occupation requires the theoretical and practical application of highly specialized knowledge **and** attainment of a bachelor's or higher degree in a **directly related specific specialty** (or its equivalent) as a minimum for entry. The position must also meet one of these criteria:

- The degree in a directly related specialty is **normally the minimum entry requirement** for the occupation;
- Such a degree is **normally required in parallel positions** among similar employers in the industry;
- **The employer normally requires** such a degree for the role; or
- The duties are so **specialized and complex** that the knowledge required is associated with a bachelor's or higher degree in the specialty.

The word "directly related" matters: generic or overly broad degree requirements are a frequent RFE trigger. See our [H-1B hub](/h1b) for more on eligibility.

## How long you have to respond to an H-1B RFE

USCIS typically gives up to **87 calendar days** to respond to an RFE. Key points:

- Extensions are **uncommon** — treat the deadline as firm.
- **Failure to respond usually results in denial.**
- One complete, well-organized response is far stronger than a partial one; USCIS decides on the record you submit.

Because RFEs add weeks or months, factor them into your planning. Track current adjudication speed on our [H-1B processing times page](/uscis-processing-times/h1b-visa) and compare across forms on the [USCIS processing times hub](/uscis-processing-times).

## How to respond to an H-1B RFE effectively

1. **Read the RFE carefully** and identify every issue raised — respond to each one.
2. **Document the specialty occupation** with a detailed job description, degree-requirement evidence, expert opinion letters, and industry comparisons.
3. **Prove the employer-employee relationship**, especially for client-site or remote work (contracts, work orders, org charts).
4. **Align wage and duties** — show the offered wage matches the LCA wage level and the complexity of the role.
5. **Confirm beneficiary qualifications** with transcripts, evaluations, and experience letters.
6. **Submit before the deadline** as one complete package.

Consider working with an experienced immigration attorney for complex or third-party placement cases.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does an H-1B RFE mean my petition will be denied?
No. An RFE is a request for more information, not a denial. Reported post-RFE approval rates stay in the mid-80% range when responses are thorough and on time.

### How long do I have to respond to an H-1B RFE in 2026?
USCIS generally allows up to 87 calendar days. Extensions are rare, and missing the deadline typically leads to denial.

### What is the most common reason for an H-1B RFE?
Specialty occupation questions are the most frequent, followed by employer-employee relationship and beneficiary qualification issues.

### Does the $100,000 H-1B fee apply to my petition?
The September 2025 proclamation's $100,000 payment applies mainly to certain new petitions for beneficiaries outside the U.S. without a valid H-1B, filed on or after September 21, 2025. Many in-country extensions and amendments are treated differently — confirm your specific scenario with counsel.

### Will the weighted selection rule change my chances?
For the FY 2027 cap (effective February 27, 2026), selection favors higher-paid, higher-skilled roles, which also raises the importance of wage-level documentation in any later RFE.
